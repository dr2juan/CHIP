# Grant: NIOSH Pilot — Heat + Ozone Co-exposure

**Focus:** Characterizing compound heat + ozone exposure risk for outdoor workers in the El Paso border region.

---

## Key Papers

### E016 — Rai et al. 2023 | *Environmental International*
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.envint.2023.107825

**Use in:** Significance, Specific Aims, Innovation

> "In 482 cities across 24 countries, heat combined with air pollution significantly exceeded mortality risk from either hazard alone — supporting integrated heat-ozone monitoring for occupational settings."

- 482 cities, 24 countries; cardiorespiratory mortality endpoint
- Combined exposure risk > either hazard alone
- Best citation for establishing co-exposure as a distinct, more dangerous risk category

---

### E017 — Oh et al. 2026 | *Environmental Research*
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.envres.2026.125027

**Use in:** Significance, Background

> "Concurrent heatwave + high ozone days carried significantly higher mortality risk than either hazard alone — establishing compound exposure as a distinct, more dangerous risk category."

- Korean cities; most recent co-exposure + mortality study
- Pair with US/border data (E010) to establish local relevance

---

### Supporting: E010 — Carrillo et al. 2017 | *Environmental Research*
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.envres.2017.11.057

> "Elevated PM2.5 and ozone at the US-Mexico border are significantly associated with increased asthma rates — highlighting the compounding air quality burden facing El Paso communities."

- US-Mexico border specific; links border air quality to health outcomes
- Use to localize the co-exposure argument to El Paso

---

### Supporting: E006 — Gibb et al. 2024 | *Annual Review of Public Health*
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1146/annurev-publhealth-060222-034715

> "Extreme heat is now one of the leading occupational hazards, with outdoor and agricultural workers facing disproportionate risk — a threat that is expanding in both duration and geography due to climate change."

- Broad significance anchor for heat as occupational hazard

---

### Supporting: E027 — Romanello et al. 2024 | *The Lancet*
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1016/S0140-6736(24)01822-1

> "In 2023, heat exposure cost the world a record 512 billion labor hours worth $835 billion in lost income — with low-income countries bearing 7.6% of GDP in losses."

- Economic burden stat — excellent for opening paragraph of Significance
