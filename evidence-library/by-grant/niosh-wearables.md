# Grant: NIOSH Pilot — Wearable Monitoring for Worker Heat Stress

**Focus:** Wearable sensor technology to detect and prevent heat-related illness in outdoor workers (co-PI role).

---

## Key Papers

### E018 ★ — Friedrich et al. 2025 | *BMC Public Health*
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1186/s12889-025-24262-2

**Use in:** Significance, Innovation, Specific Aims, Gap Statement

> "A scoping review found wearable interventions for heat-exposed outdoor workers are feasible but effectiveness evidence remains limited — highlighting the gap this grant aims to fill."

- Most recent and directly relevant citation (2025 scoping review)
- Establishes feasibility + evidence gap simultaneously — ideal for Significance
- Use as the anchor "gap statement" citation

---

### E019 — Buller MJ et al. 2017 | *Journal of Applied Physiology*
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1152/japplphysiol.00353.2017

**Use in:** Innovation, Approach, Preliminary Data

> "Wearable physiological monitoring can accurately estimate core body temperature from heart rate — enabling real-time detection of dangerous heat strain in workers without invasive measurement."

- Key methods paper: validates the physiological monitoring algorithm approach
- Cite in Approach when describing the sensor/algorithm rationale

---

### E020 — Sugg MM et al. 2019 | *International Journal of Biometeorology*
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1007/s00484-019-01833-8

**Use in:** Innovation, Human Subjects, Community Engagement section

> "Outdoor workers accepted wearable heat sensors but cited privacy and employer surveillance as key concerns — critical community engagement considerations for wearable monitoring grant design."

- Essential for Human Subjects / Ethical Considerations
- Shows you're aware of and addressing worker concerns in your design
- Supports participatory/worker-centered framing

---

### Supporting: E007 — Tustin et al. 2018 | *MMWR* (CDC/NIOSH)
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.15585/mmwr.mm6726a1

> "CDC/NIOSH surveillance data confirm that current occupational heat exposure limits are insufficient — heat-related illness continues to occur at recommended threshold levels."

- Regulatory/policy context for why better monitoring tools are needed

---

### Supporting: E008 — Heinzerling et al. 2020 | *Am J Industrial Medicine*
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1002/ajim.23191

> "Undocumented status, inadequate acclimatization, and high exertion are independent HRI risk factors — a profile that mirrors conditions for border-region outdoor workers."

- Risk factor evidence linking to your target population
