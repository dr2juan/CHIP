# Grant: NIMHD — VALE+Tú (Day Laborer Environmental Health)

**Focus:** Environmental health disparities among day laborers (jornaleros) in El Paso; community-based participatory research (CBPR) model.

---

## Key Papers

### E024 — Salinas JL, Salinas M 2022 | *Journal of Migration and Health*
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jmh.2022.100106

**Use in:** Significance, Health Disparities Framework, Background

> "Undocumented Latino migrant laborers were designated 'essential workers' while systemic racism left them without workplace protections or healthcare access — a structural gap requiring targeted occupational health research."

- Provides the structural/social determinants framework (systemic racism → occupational risk)
- COVID context makes disparities visible; applies directly to ongoing conditions
- Use to frame why this population requires NIMHD-focused health disparities research

---

### E025 — Williams Q et al. 2010 | *Journal of Safety Research*
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jsr.2010.02.009

**Use in:** Innovation, Approach, CBPR Methods, Track Record

> "After peer-led participatory training, Latino day laborers significantly increased PPE use — and 66% shared safety information with coworkers, amplifying reach beyond the initial cohort."

- Directly demonstrates CBPR effectiveness with Latino day laborer population
- 66% peer diffusion stat is highly quotable for demonstrating community reach
- Establishes precedent for the peer-facilitated model VALE+Tú likely employs

---

### E026 — Leclere OA, López RA 2012 | *Journal of Immigrant and Minority Health*
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1007/s10903-011-9516-z

**Use in:** Significance, Background, Health Disparities

> "Latino jornaleros work in occupations that are 'unmonitored, unprotected, and often unsafe' with no healthcare beyond emergency rooms — illustrating the structural gap in occupational health coverage for this population."

- Uses the term "jornalero" — directly matches VALE+Tú target population framing
- Strong qualitative evidence of structural exclusion from occupational protections
- The "unmonitored, unprotected, and often unsafe" quote is ready to cite verbatim

---

### E027 — Romanello M et al. 2024 | *The Lancet*
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1016/S0140-6736(24)01822-1

**Use in:** Significance, Broader Impacts, Economic Justification

> "In 2023, heat exposure cost the world a record 512 billion labor hours worth $835 billion in lost income — with low-income countries bearing 7.6% of GDP in losses."

- Global burden stat; use to establish macro-level economic and health equity stakes
- Low-income country burden → analogous to low-income border worker burden

---

### Supporting: E008 — Heinzerling et al. 2020 | *Am J Industrial Medicine*
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1002/ajim.23191

> "Undocumented status, inadequate acclimatization, and high exertion are independent HRI risk factors in California agricultural workers — a profile that mirrors conditions for border-region outdoor workers."

- Links undocumented/immigrant status directly to HRI risk
- California context translates well to Texas/El Paso

---

### Supporting: E009 ★ — Perkison et al. 2024 | *J Occup Environ Med* (UTHealth SWCOEH)
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1097/JOM.0000000000003051

> "Over 9 years, the UTHealth SWCOEH HIPP program achieved sustained reductions in heat-related illness in Texas workers."

- UTHealth institutional track record — directly relevant if SWCOEH is a partner

---

### Supporting: E010 — Carrillo et al. 2017 | *Environmental Research*
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.envres.2017.11.057

> "Elevated PM2.5 and ozone at the US-Mexico border are significantly associated with increased asthma — highlighting the compounding air quality burden facing El Paso communities."

- Border-specific environmental exposure data; use for local context
